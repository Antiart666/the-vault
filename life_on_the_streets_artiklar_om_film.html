LIFE ON THE STREETS

Källa: Artiklar om film

LIFE ON THE STREETS Ten years later, Stefan Jarl once again ventured into the lives of Kenta and Stoffe, and made a sequel, A Decent Life, in 1979. Kenta has tried to enter society. He's got a job and lives with his girlfriend, Eva and their son, Patrik. Stoffe, on the other hand, has gone from bad to worse. He has a hardcore drug habit that has brought him to the brink of physical collapse. Kenta and Stoffe don't socialize anymore, but director Jarl sets up a meeting. At first they have a good time, but they once again get into a quarrel. Some weeks later, Stoffe dies of a drug overdose. But A Decent Life contains much more that just the story of Kenta and Stoffe. Jarl seems to have vacuumed the underground in Stockholm and shows with an unflinching gaze the hell that junkies and prostitutes endure every day. We're invited to witness some extremely disturbing scenes at the toilets of Stockholm's Grand Central Station. Many of the junkies look like old people already, even though most are still well under thirty. Against this backdrop, Stoffe's tragic death is grimly prescient. A Decent Life received critical acclaim. Some critics claimed that Jarl tampered a bit too much with his subjects, ending up with a somewhat manipulated or semi-fictional film. Jarl admitted that the film contained some arranged scenes, arguing that the camera couldn't always be in place and therefore some re-enactment was necessary. In the twenty years since its release, A Decent Life has lost none of its vitality or sting and ranks as one of the most effective European non-fiction films ever.
